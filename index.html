Understanding the Problem

What is an internet blackout?

The internet isn't one thing. It's thousands of separate networks all agreeing to pass traffic to each other. When you visit a website hosted in another country, your request hops through multiple networks to get there.

Think of it this way

The internet is like a city of buildings connected by roads. Each building (network) has its own rooms and people. The roads let traffic flow between buildings. Normally, if one road closes, you can take another route.

Most countries have multiple connections to the global internet. This redundancy makes the internet resilient. If one path fails, traffic finds another way.

In Iran, this redundancy has been systematically eliminated. All international traffic flows through one single point—making a nationwide blackout trivially easy to execute.

The Vulnerability

A single point of failure for 87 million people

All of Iran's international internet traffic passes through one gateway: AS49666, operated by the state-controlled Telecommunication Infrastructure Company (TIC).

What's happening now

The gateway has withdrawn its BGP route announcements. Global routers no longer know how to reach Iran. Traffic has nowhere to go—Iran has been erased from the internet.

The Technical Reality

How a blackout actually happens

The internet uses BGP (Border Gateway Protocol) to know how to route traffic. When a network wants to be reachable, it "announces" its addresses to neighbors.

During a blackout, Iranian networks stop announcing their addresses. The rest of the world's routers forget how to reach Iran. It's not blocking—it's erasure.
